Prompt engineering with CRIT

We used the CRIT framework to turn rough ideas into clearer prompts: Context ¬∑ Role ¬∑ Interview ¬∑ Task.

C ¬∑ Context

Tell the AI what you‚Äôre working on: your class, your project, your community partner, and any constraints (timeline, audience, format).

R ¬∑ Role

Assign the AI a useful role: research assistant, skeptical editor, tutor, outreach coach, grant-writing helper, etc.

I ¬∑ Interview

Treat it like a back-and-forth: ask follow-up questions, ask it to challenge you, or to suggest non-obvious directions and gaps.

T ¬∑ Task

Be specific about what you want: ‚Äúturn these notes into 3 research questions,‚Äù ‚Äúsummarize in bullet points,‚Äù ‚Äúdraft 2 email options,‚Äù etc.

Example CRIT prompt: I‚Äôm a college student working with a community partner focused on [TOPIC]. Context: This is for a semester-long project where we need to understand their challenges and propose realistic ideas. Role: Act as a research assistant who explains things in plain language. Interview: Ask me 3‚Äì4 questions about my partner and what I already know before you start. Task: Turn my rough notes into 3‚Äì5 clear research questions and suggest keywords I can use in academic databases.

Using AI ethically: guardrails we discussed

AI is powerful, but it also has limits. It can hallucinate, reflect bias, and encourage ‚Äúcognitive offloading‚Äù where you stop thinking. Here are the key guardrails from the workshop:

Green light ¬∑ low-risk uses

Brainstorming ideas, organizing notes, generating questions, explaining concepts in a new way, summarizing non-sensitive readings.

Yellow light ¬∑ use with caution

Drafting emails, writing outlines, proposing recommendations. You must edit, fact-check, and make the final decisions yourself.

Red light ¬∑ high-risk uses

Copy-pasting assignments, sharing private data about real people, using AI to deceive, or letting it replace your own thinking entirely.

  • Double-check all facts. AI has no built-in concept of truth.
  • Protect privacy. Don‚Äôt paste full names, IDs, or sensitive stories into prompts.
  • Watch for bias. AI reflects patterns in its training data‚Äîuse your critical thinking.
  • You are the manager. The final work is your responsibility, not the model‚Äôs.

NotebookLM: deeper research & learning

We looked at NotebookLM as an example of using AI on your own sources (RAG). You can load readings, notes, or documents and then ask questions about them.

  • Lower hallucinations when you stay within your uploaded sources.
  • Can generate podcasts, overviews, and mind maps from your materials.
  • Helps you quiz yourself and explore connections across documents.

Still: always check the sources and verify anything important before you rely on it.
